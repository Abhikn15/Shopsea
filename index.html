An Extraordinary Place

An Archipelago
Like No Other

Spread across 800 kilometres of the Bay of Bengal, the Andaman and Nicobar Islands form one of India's most astonishing island chains. With 572 islands, islets and rocky outcrops — only 37 of which are inhabited — this is a place where the modern world feels very far away, and the ancient rhythms of sea and forest still govern daily life.

The waters here shift from turquoise to cobalt as the ocean deepens. The beaches are blanketed in white coral sand, fringed by mangroves and rainforests untouched for millennia. Beneath the surface lie some of Asia's most biodiverse coral reefs — home to sea turtles, manta rays, whale sharks and over 500 species of fish.

It is one of the last places on Earth where the shoreline holds its original wildness. And it is precisely this wildness — unmediated, unglamoured — that lives in every piece Sagaré creates.

The People

Ancient Tribes &
Island Artisans

The Andaman Islands are home to some of the most ancient and isolated indigenous communities on the planet — the Great Andamanese, Onge, Jarawa, Sentinelese, Nicobarese and Shompen peoples have lived here for an estimated 60,000 years, making the Andamans one of the oldest continuously inhabited places outside Africa.

Sagaré works with artisans from coastal fishing communities whose craft traditions have been shaped by centuries of living beside the sea. They know which shells carry the finest lustre, which driftwood holds the most beautiful grain, and how to coax beauty from natural materials without diminishing their wildness.

  • Artisans trained across 3+ generations in shell, bead & pearl craft
  • Sustainable harvesting — only what the shore freely gives
  • Fair, direct wages — no middlemen between Sagaré and the maker
  • Hand tools only — no industrial processes whatsoever
  • Every piece individually made; natural variations mark authenticity
  • Seasonal production following the natural rhythms of the shore
The Pearl

The Andaman Sea
as Material

The Andaman Sea is not merely a backdrop — it is the material itself. Shells develop their colours over years in these specific warm, mineral-rich waters. No two cowries are identical. No two cones carry the same spiral. That irreproducibility is what makes Sagaré pieces impossible to truly replicate.

Andaman sea pearls — cultivated in the island's protected lagoons — are among the finest in Asia for their subtle iridescence and natural body colour. Unlike heavily processed freshwater pearls, Andaman pearls are finished with minimal intervention, preserving the luminous quality that the sea gave them.

  • Cultivated in the Andaman Sea's warm, clear lagoons
  • Subtle iridescence — the true lustre of island waters
  • Minimal processing — natural finish preserved
  • Sizes 6mm to 14mm, each individually selected by hand
  • Every pearl carries provenance from its island of origin

When to Come
to the Islands

The Andaman Islands have a tropical monsoon climate. The best season is October to April — calm seas, clear skies, and the island markets in full swing. Port Blair's Aberdeen Bazaar is where island craft is most alive, and where Sagaré's world is most visible to those who come in person.

History & Heritage

A History as
Deep as the Sea

The Andaman Islands have been inhabited for at least 60,000 years, making them among the earliest continuously occupied lands outside Africa. For most of this extraordinary span, the islands' indigenous people lived in complete isolation — perfectly adapted to their island world, with no need for the outside and no contact with it.

The islands entered a darker chapter during British colonial rule, when the Cellular Jail — known as Kala Pani — was built in Port Blair in 1906 to house Indian freedom fighters. Today this structure stands as a national monument, its evening light-and-sound show a sobering account of the islands' complex relationship with mainland India.

After independence in 1947, the islands became a Union Territory and gradually opened to settlement. The blending of mainland Indian communities with original island craft traditions created the distinctive material culture Sagaré draws from today — ancient in its materials and forms, yet entirely alive and still evolving with every tide.
